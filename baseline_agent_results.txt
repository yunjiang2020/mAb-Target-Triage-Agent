TARGET: HER2
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HER2 (Human Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor 2, also known as ERBB2) is an
exceptionally suitable and clinically validated target for Antibody-Drug
Conjugate (ADC) development. In fact, it is one of the most successful ADC
targets to date, with multiple approved ADCs on the market.

Here's an assessment based on standard ADC target criteria:

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue):
   - Tumor Expression: Highly overexpressed in a significant subset of various
     cancers, most notably breast cancer (15-20%), gastric/gastroesophageal
     junction cancer (15-20%), and also ovarian, lung, bladder, colon, and
     others. Typically associated with aggressive disease and poorer prognosis.
   - Normal Tissue Expression: Generally low or absent in most normal adult
     tissues, but expressed at low levels in heart, kidney, lung, liver, and
     gut epithelium — a key consideration for on-target, off-tumor toxicity.
   - Homogeneity: Can be heterogeneous within a tumor and across metastatic
     sites, but "HER2-positive" is well-defined and "HER2-low" is now a target.
   - Internalization: Transmembrane receptor that undergoes receptor-mediated
     endocytosis upon antibody binding — crucial for ADC payload delivery.

2. Function:
   - Oncogenic Driver: A receptor tyrosine kinase that, when overexpressed,
     signals constitutively, driving proliferation, survival, angiogenesis, and
     metastasis. A well-established oncogenic driver.

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Excellent Druggability: mAbs like trastuzumab (Herceptin) and pertuzumab
     (Perjeta) have revolutionized HER2-positive cancer treatment.
   - Existing ADCs: Trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1, Kadcyla) and trastuzumab
     deruxtecan (T-DXd, Enhertu) validate HER2 as a premier ADC target.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - Highly Validated: Routinely tested in breast and gastric cancers by IHC
     and FISH; a critical predictive and prognostic biomarker.
   - Established Therapies: mAbs, TKIs, and ADCs all exist.

5. Safety Considerations (On-Target, Off-Tumor Toxicity):
   - Cardiac Toxicity: A known class effect (e.g., left ventricular dysfunction)
     from low-level HER2 expression in cardiomyocytes.
   - Pulmonary Toxicity: With T-DXd, interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis is a
     serious, sometimes fatal adverse event.
   - Other: Myelosuppression, GI toxicities, peripheral neuropathy (payload-
     dependent).

6. Mechanisms of Resistance:
   - HER2 mutations, bypass signaling (PI3K/AKT/mTOR, MAPK), loss of HER2
     expression, or truncated receptors. New ADCs and combinations aim to
     overcome these.

Conclusion:
HER2 is an outstanding ADC target. Its high and differential expression in
aggressive cancers, established oncogenic role, excellent internalization, and
extensive clinical validation (including approved ADCs T-DM1 and T-DXd) make it
highly attractive. While low-level normal-tissue expression requires monitoring
for cardiac/pulmonary toxicities, the therapeutic index has proven favorable for
many patients. Research continues on linker-payload optimization and expansion
to "HER2-low" populations.

TARGET: CD19
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CD19 is a highly suitable and clinically validated target for Antibody-Drug
Conjugate (ADC) development, particularly in B-cell malignancies. It has a
strong track record, not only with an approved ADC but also as the primary
target for several groundbreaking CAR T-cell therapies.

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue):
   - Tumor Expression: A pan-B cell marker expressed on virtually all B-cell
     lineage neoplasms — non-Hodgkin lymphomas (DLBCL, follicular, mantle
     cell), ALL, CLL. Typically uniform and high on malignant B cells.
   - Normal Tissue Expression: Expressed exclusively on normal B cells
     (pre-B through mature), absent on plasma cells, hematopoietic stem cells,
     and non-lymphoid tissues.
   - Homogeneity: Generally homogeneous across malignant B cells.
   - Internalization: Rapidly internalizes upon antibody binding.

2. Function:
   - B-cell Co-receptor: A key component of the B-cell co-receptor complex
     regulating B-cell development, activation, proliferation, and
     differentiation. Not a direct oncogenic driver, but an excellent marker.

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Highly Druggable.
   - Existing ADCs: Loncastuximab tesirine (Zynlonta) is approved for
     relapsed/refractory DLBCL.
   - Extensive Prior Validation (CAR T-cells): The success of CD19 CAR T-cell
     therapies (tisagenlecleucel, axicabtagene ciloleucel, brexucabtagene
     autoleucel) profoundly validates CD19.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - Routinely assessed by flow cytometry and IHC for B-cell leukemia/lymphoma
     diagnosis. Presence predicts eligibility for CD19-targeted therapies.

5. Safety Considerations (On-Target, Off-Tumor Toxicity):
   - B-cell Aplasia: The main on-target, off-tumor effect — expected and
     manageable; can cause hypogammaglobulinemia (may need Ig replacement);
     generally reversible.
   - Payload-Specific Toxicities: For loncastuximab tesirine (a PBD-dimer DNA
     minor groove binder): myelosuppression, hepatotoxicity, effusions.

6. Mechanisms of Resistance:
   - Antigen loss/downregulation, CD19 splice variants, lineage switching
     (e.g., to myeloid), and payload-specific resistance.

Conclusion:
CD19 is an excellent and highly compelling ADC target for B-cell malignancies.
Robust, uniform, high expression plus rapid internalization make it ideal for
payload delivery, and the success of both CAR T-cell therapies and an approved
ADC strongly validate it. B-cell aplasia is a predictable, manageable trade-off,
and the therapeutic index is generally favorable in the appropriate population.

TARGET: PSMA
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PSMA (Prostate-Specific Membrane Antigen, also known as FOLH1) is a highly
promising and rapidly emerging target for Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC)
development, particularly in prostate cancer. Its clinical validation through
imaging and radioligand therapy has paved the way for its exploration in the
ADC space.

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue):
   - Tumor Expression: Robustly overexpressed on prostate cancer cells
     (~90-100%), especially advanced metastatic castration-resistant disease;
     also on the neovasculature of various other solid tumors.
   - Normal Tissue Expression: Restricted and lower than in prostate cancer.
     Key sites: kidney (proximal renal tubule brush border), salivary glands,
     small intestine, some peripheral nerves/ganglia.
   - Internalization: Efficiently internalizes upon ligand or antibody binding.

2. Function:
   - Carboxypeptidase Activity: A Type II transmembrane glycoprotein / folate
     hydrolase (NAALADase). Robust overexpression in prostate cancer makes it
     an excellent marker for targeted therapy.

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Highly Druggable (large accessible extracellular domain).
   - Radioligand Therapy: Lutetium-177 PSMA-617 (Pluvicto) is a monumental
     validation in mCRPC.
   - Imaging: Ga-68 and F-18 PSMA PET tracers confirm high expression.
   - ADCs in Development: Several in clinical development; no approved PSMA ADC
     yet, but the field is very active.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - Exceptionally well-validated as a diagnostic and therapeutic target;
     PSMA PET is a powerful predictive biomarker.

5. Safety Considerations (On-Target, Off-Tumor Toxicity):
   - Xerostomia (dry mouth) from salivary gland uptake; nephrotoxicity from
     renal expression; GI toxicity; myelosuppression; possible neuropathy.
   - Therapeutic Window: The key challenge; the success of RLT suggests a window
     exists.

6. Mechanisms of Resistance:
   - Antigen loss/downregulation, heterogeneity, splice variants, and
     payload-specific resistance.

Conclusion:
PSMA is an exceptionally attractive and highly validated ADC target for prostate
cancer, with potential in other solid tumors. High differential expression,
efficient internalization, and the success of PSMA imaging and radioligand
therapy provide strong support. The main challenge is managing on-target,
off-tumor toxicities in salivary glands and kidneys; ongoing ADC development
aims to optimize linker-payload combinations to improve the therapeutic index.

TARGET: MUC16
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
MUC16 (Mucin 16, also known as CA125) is a challenging but clinically validated
and promising target for Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC) development, primarily in
ovarian cancer. Its complex biology, including extensive glycosylation and
shedding, presents unique hurdles, but the recent approval of an ADC targeting
MUC16 demonstrates its potential.

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue):
   - Tumor Expression: Highly expressed on most (80-90%) epithelial ovarian
     cancers; also in pancreatic, NSCLC, endometrial, and colon cancers. A very
     large (up to 2500 kDa), heavily glycosylated transmembrane mucin.
   - Normal Tissue Expression: Limited — fallopian tube, endometrium,
     endocervix, conjunctiva, tracheobronchial epithelium, kidney tubules.
   - Homogeneity: Can be heterogeneous within a tumor and across sites.
   - Internalization: Historically debated; slower due to size and dense
     glycosylation, but sufficient for ADC activity.

2. Function:
   - Physical barrier/protection; anti-adhesive (promotes dissemination);
     immunosuppression (e.g., via mesothelin on macrophages); emerging
     signaling roles. CA125, its shed extracellular domain, is a serum
     biomarker and a potential "antigen sink."

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Druggable (large accessible extracellular domain).
   - Existing ADCs: Mirvetuximab soravtansine (Elahere) is approved for
     platinum-resistant ovarian cancer with high folate receptor-alpha (FRalpha)
     expression — a major validation for MUC16.
   - Challenges: Antigen shedding (soluble CA125 sponges up ADC); heavy
     glycosylation can hinder epitope access.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - CA125 is a long-standing serum biomarker; mirvetuximab soravtansine's
     approval (FRalpha-positive, platinum-resistant ovarian cancer) provides
     strong validation.

5. Safety Considerations (On-Target, Off-Tumor Toxicity):
   - Ocular Toxicity: Conjunctival/corneal expression drives blurred vision,
     dry eye, keratopathy (prominent with mirvetuximab soravtansine).
   - Payload-Specific (DM4, tubulin inhibitor): myelosuppression, GI toxicity,
     peripheral neuropathy.

6. Mechanisms of Resistance:
   - Antigen loss/downregulation, antigen shedding (CA125 sink), suboptimal
     internalization, payload-specific resistance, bypass signaling.

Conclusion:
MUC16 is a clinically validated and important ADC target, particularly for
ovarian cancer (per the approval of mirvetuximab soravtansine). High, relatively
selective expression plus sufficient internalization make it attractive, but
antigen shedding (CA125 sink), tumor heterogeneity, and ocular toxicity remain
challenges. Next-generation MUC16 ADCs aim to optimize linkers, payloads, and
shedding-resistance to improve the therapeutic index.

TARGET: P53
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
P53 (tumor protein p53, also known as TP53) is NOT a suitable target for
Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC) development in the traditional sense.

Here's a breakdown of why, and a discussion of its importance in cancer biology
and alternative targeting strategies:

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue) — The Primary Issue:
   - Intracellular Location: P53 is a nuclear transcription factor. ADCs bind
     cell-surface antigens and internalize; P53 in the nucleus is inaccessible
     to a circulating antibody.
   - Ubiquitous Expression: Wild-type P53 is expressed in virtually all normal
     cells; targeting it directly would be catastrophically toxic.
   - Tumor Expression: Often mutated (loss-of-function, >50% of cancers — no
     "normal" P53 to target) or wild-type but suppressed (e.g., by MDM2/MDMX).

2. Function:
   - Guardian of the Genome: Senses stress and triggers cell-cycle arrest, DNA
     repair, senescence, or apoptosis. Not a cell-surface receptor.

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Not Druggable by ADCs (intracellular).
   - Historically "undruggable" by small molecules, but changing: mutant-p53
     reactivators (e.g., APR-246/eprenetapopt), MDM2/MDMX inhibitors (nutlins,
     idasanutlin), and gene therapy approaches.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - P53 mutational status is a well-established prognostic/predictive
     biomarker. No ADC validation, because ADCs cannot access it.

5. Safety Considerations:
   - If targeted by an ADC, the ubiquitous, vital role of wild-type P53 would
     cause catastrophic, lethal systemic toxicity.

Conclusion:
P53, despite being arguably the most critical tumor suppressor in human cancer,
is absolutely not a suitable ADC target — fundamentally because of its
intracellular (nuclear) location. Its importance has instead driven small-
molecule and gene-therapy strategies to restore or unleash its function.

TARGET: HLA-C
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HLA-C is NOT a suitable target for Antibody-Drug Conjugate (ADC) development in
the traditional sense, and in fact would be highly problematic and likely lead
to lethal systemic toxicity.

1. Expression Profile (Tumor vs. Normal Tissue):
   - Tumor Expression: An MHC Class I molecule on most nucleated cells,
     including tumor cells — but frequently downregulated by tumors to evade
     cytotoxic T lymphocytes, hurting ADC efficacy.
   - Normal Tissue Expression: THE insurmountable problem — ubiquitously
     expressed on virtually ALL nucleated cells (only red blood cells
     typically lack MHC Class I).
   - Implication: A cytotoxic payload against a near-universal antigen causes
     devastating, lethal on-target, off-tumor toxicity.
   - Internalization: Ubiquity overrides any benefit from internalization.

2. Function:
   - Antigen Presentation: Presents intracellular peptides to CD8+ CTLs.
   - NK Cell Regulation: A KIR ligand signaling "self" to inhibit NK cells.
   - Not a growth receptor / oncogenic driver; relevant to immune modulation,
     not cytotoxic payload delivery.

3. Druggability & Antibody Development:
   - Antibodies exist (research/diagnostics), but ubiquitous expression makes
     HLA-C unsuitable for ADC payload delivery (would kill all nucleated cells).
   - Relevant instead to other immunotherapy strategies.

4. Clinical Validation & Biomarkers:
   - A biomarker of immune context (expression/loss predicts immunotherapy
     response). No ADC validation or development, due to the safety issue.

5. Safety Considerations (On-Target, Off-Tumor Toxicity):
   - Catastrophic and lethal: severe myelosuppression (marrow aplasia),
     multi-organ failure, immunosuppression, death. An unacceptable, untreatable
     toxicity profile.

6. Mechanisms of Resistance:
   - (Hypothetically) Tumors already downregulate HLA-C to evade T cells,
     further shrinking any already-impossible therapeutic window.

Conclusion:
HLA-C is completely unsuitable as an ADC target. Its ubiquitous expression on
nearly all normal nucleated cells would cause catastrophic, lethal systemic
toxicity, making a therapeutic window impossible. It is important in immune
biology and other immunotherapy strategies, but presents an insurmountable
barrier for ADC-mediated cytotoxic delivery.
